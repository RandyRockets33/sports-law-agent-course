Agent Practice · Module 05 · ⏱ 60 min

Fiduciary Duties, Conflicts, and Ethics

Loyalty, care, candor. Conflict patterns. Vulnerable client. Referral-fee discipline.

Open library My account

Module overview

The agent's fiduciary duties are the most important non-technical content in this course. By the end of this module you will be able to articulate the agent's duties of loyalty, care, candor, confidentiality, and avoidance of conflicts, and you will be able to identify the recurring conflict patterns and the protocols for handling them.

Learning objectives

  1. Define the agent's fiduciary duties.
  2. Identify the recurring conflict patterns (multi-client, family-member, lawyer-agent, third-party-payor, business-transaction).
  3. Apply the disclosure-and-consent framework to recurring conflicts.
  4. Identify the ethical pitfalls specific to athlete-side practice (vulnerable client, referral fees, family entanglement).

Practical exercise

The instructor provides three short fact patterns (each two paragraphs). For each: - Identify the conflict (if any). - State the disclosure that must be made. - State whether the conflict is waivable; if so, with what protections. - State whether the agent should accept the representation, decline it, or accept with conditions.

Sample fact patterns (instructor may use these or substitute): - Pattern A: The agent's agency already represents the starting quarterback of an NFL team. A backup quarterback on the same team asks the agency to represent him for an upcoming contract negotiation. - Pattern B: The athlete's mother offers to "help" the agent recruit other clients in the athlete's community in exchange for a portion of the agent's commissions from those clients. - Pattern C: A financial advisor offers the agent a 20% referral fee for every athlete-client the agent steers to the advisor.

Rubric (10 points): Identification of conflict (3), Disclosure framing (3), Waiver analysis (2), Recommendation (2).

Discussion prompt

The course identifies referral fees as a common ethical failure. Yet referral fees are pervasive in adjacent industries (real estate, financial services, professional services). Why are they more problematic in the agent-athlete context than in other professional contexts? What structural features of the agent-athlete relationship make referral fees particularly harmful?

Supplemental reading
