College Sport After Alston and House

NIL statutes, NCAA policy, House revenue-sharing, transfer portal, F-1 visa overlay.

Open library My account

Module overview

This is the module on the post-Alston and post-House college economy. By the end of this module you will know the antitrust foundation, the NIL architecture, the revenue-sharing settlement framework, and the practical agent landscape for college athletes.

Learning objectives

  1. Understand NCAA v. Alston (2021) as the foundational case for college-athlete compensation.
  2. Identify the state NIL statutes and how they interact with NCAA NIL policy.
  3. Understand the House v. NCAA settlement and the revenue-sharing framework.
  4. Identify the role of NIL collectives and their tax/employment/regulatory implications.
  5. Identify the remaining doctrinal frontiers (employee status, federal NIL legislation, Title IX).

Q12 · Short answer
In two to three sentences, explain why the boundary between permissible NIL compensation and impermissible recruiting inducement is the single most contested NCAA enforcement question.
Model answer: NIL deals are permitted; recruiting inducements are not. But many NIL deals look — and economically function — like recruiting inducements, particularly when paid by collectives associated with a specific institution to a prospective student-athlete who has not yet enrolled. The NCAA has attempted to enforce a distinction, but the *Tennessee and Virginia v. NCAA* preliminary injunction has limited enforcement, and the doctrinal landscape remains unsettled.

Practical exercise

A five-star high school football recruit committed to your state's flagship university is in his senior year of high school. He has multiple NIL deal offers, including: a deal with a national apparel brand worth $200K over two years, a deal with a local car dealership worth $50K over one year, and a deal with the institution's NIL collective worth $300K over the first year of enrollment. The athlete's family has no business experience.

Outline: - Compliance review steps you would take on each deal. - Tax planning structure you would recommend. - Family-system structure for handling the money. - Reporting obligations to the institution. - Risk analysis for each deal.

Rubric (20 points): Compliance (5), Tax (4), Family structure (4), Reporting (3), Risk analysis (4).

Discussion prompt

The House v. NCAA settlement establishes revenue-sharing as part of the college economy. Some critics argue this transforms college athletics into a quasi-employment relationship and may trigger reclassification under FLSA and NLRA. Other critics argue the settlement does not go far enough. What is your position on whether college athletes should be classified as employees? What are the practical implications for the agent's practice if they are?

Supplemental reading

"The post-Alston and post-House environments have produced new gender-equity questions. NIL income is private and not directly an institutional Title IX matter. But institutional facilitation of NIL deals, institutional-collective coordination, and revenue-sharing payments may all trigger Title IX analysis." — Volume I, § 25.6.
