The Institutional Architecture

NFL, MLB, NBA, NHL — structural differences that drive every contract decision.

Open library My account

Module overview

This module gives you a working map of the institutional architecture of organized sport in the United States. By the end of this module you will be able to identify the structural differences among the four major leagues, the second-tier and emerging leagues, the college sport architecture, and the Olympic / national governing body system. These structural differences drive enormous practical consequence for the agent — different leagues mean different CBAs, different draft rules, different salary structures, different free-agency mechanics, different discipline regimes.

Learning objectives

  1. Describe the structural differences among the NFL, MLB, NBA, and NHL.
  2. Identify the second-tier and emerging leagues (MLS, WNBA, MMA/UFC, golf, tennis, boxing).
  3. Describe the NCAA Division I architecture, the Power Four / Group of Five conference system, and the post-2024 realignment landscape.
  4. Identify the Olympic and NGB system: USOPC, NGBs, SafeSport, USADA, CAS.

Q10 · Short answer
In two or three sentences, explain why the structural difference between the NFL (hard cap, mostly non-guaranteed) and MLB (no cap, mostly guaranteed) matters to the agent during contract negotiation.
Model answer: In the NFL the agent's principal lever is the signing bonus and the guarantee structure built into the addendum, because base salaries are often non-guaranteed and players can be cut. In MLB the agent's principal lever is the multi-year guaranteed base, plus performance bonuses, opt-outs, and no-trade protection, because the contract is typically fully guaranteed once signed.
