Sports Gambling and Integrity

Murphy v. NCAA. League and NCAA rules. Prop-bet exposure. Integrity firewall.

Open library My account

Module overview

This module is about the gambling-integrity regime that has expanded dramatically since Murphy v. NCAA (2018). By the end of this module you will understand the federal and state legal framework, the league and NCAA integrity policies, the prop-bet exposure that has produced the Jontay Porter and Calvin Ridley cases, and the conduct boundaries the agent must police for athletes, family members, and associates.

Learning objectives

  1. Identify the federal gambling statutes (Wire Act, Sports Bribery Act, IGBA, UIGEA).
  2. Apply Murphy v. NCAA (2018) to the post-PASPA state regulation of sports betting.
  3. Identify the league and NCAA integrity policies and their penalty architectures.
  4. Counsel athletes on gambling conduct boundaries.
  5. Counsel athletes on family-member and associate-related integrity risks.

Q12 · Short answer
Two or three sentences — explain why family-member and associate betting on the athlete's sport is a specific integrity-risk pathway the agent must police.
Model answer: An athlete with inside information (injury status, planned tactical decisions, individual fitness) can convey that information to a family member or associate who bets on the athlete's sport, creating the appearance or reality of an integrity violation even if the athlete personally has not bet. The integrity monitors look for unusual betting activity from individuals connected to athletes; this is a known investigation pathway. The agent must counsel the athlete to (a) instruct family and associates not to bet on the athlete's sport, (b) maintain firewalls on inside information, and (c) report any solicitation of impropriety immediately.

Practical exercise

A new NFL client signs a major endorsement contract with a sportsbook operator for a brand-ambassador role. The deal would pay $1 million per year for two years. The client's brother runs a fantasy-sports league with a small group of friends and occasionally places small recreational bets on NFL games.

Write a one-page memo covering: - Compliance review of the endorsement contract (league rules, state rules, contract terms). - Counseling the client on the inside-information firewall with the brother. - Family/associate integrity-conduct protocol you would establish. - Reporting and documentation structure. - Risk-of-discipline analysis if the brother is detected as a bettor.

Rubric (20 points): Contract review (5), Firewall counseling (4), Family protocol (4), Reporting structure (3), Risk analysis (4).

Discussion prompt

Some leagues have moved from prohibiting all player gambling endorsements to permitting limited sportsbook-related brand-ambassador deals. Where should the line be? Is there a workable distinction between a player endorsing a sportsbook in brand-marketing roles and a player betting on sport? What controls should be in place if such endorsements are permitted?
